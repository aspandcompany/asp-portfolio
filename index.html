Our Work

Place identity,
built from the inside.

Most businesses that belong to remarkable places don't look like it online. The photography is stock or phone-snapped. The story is missing. The place is invisible.

ASP & Co. changes that — not as an outside agency, but as someone who actually belongs. We document the intersection of culture and environment through extended presence, building visual portfolios that show what it feels like to be in a specific place, run by specific people, rooted in specific history.

The result is a living archive — imagery that grows richer over time, on retainer, as the relationship with a place deepens. Not a photoshoot. A practice.

Sponge Docks & Spring Bayou

The story of the Tarpon Springs sponge docks begins with the Bahamians — Black sponge fishermen who settled here in the late 1800s and introduced the traditional hook method, working the Gulf beds before anyone else had a commercial operation. The Greeks arrived in the early 1900s, bringing helmet diving and a more industrial approach that transformed the trade. Both communities built this place. The docks carry both histories, though one has been told louder than the other. Spring Bayou anchors the inland side — a freshwater spring ringed by Victorian architecture that makes this part of town feel like a different century.

Rose Cemetery & Tarpon's Bahamian Heritage

Rose Cemetery — also known as Rose Hill Cemetery — is the oldest and largest intact segregated African-American cemetery in Pinellas County, established in the early 1900s on North Jasmine Avenue. The Bahamian community buried here includes the hook spongers who built the trade before the Greeks arrived, community founders, church leaders, and civil rights figures. The graves tell the story plainly: conch shells from Eleuthera left on headstones, east-west burial orientation carried over from African and Caribbean tradition, cinder blocks with a single date where funding ran out for stone. This is where Black Tarpon Springs keeps its record. The Union Academy neighborhood — the epicenter of the city's Bahamian community — sits nearby, its streets the living counterpart to what the cemetery holds.

ASP & Co. — The Practice

The work begins with a question: what does this place actually feel like to the people who belong to it?

Founder portrait

Ashton Stepter — St. Petersburg, FL

ASP & Co. was founded by Ashton Stepter in St. Petersburg, Florida — not as an agency, and not as a photography business in the traditional sense. It grew out of a conviction that most places are misrepresented by the imagery that's supposed to speak for them. Stock photography flattens. Campaigns extract. What's left is a version of a place that serves the marketing brief but loses the thing that made the place worth documenting in the first place.

The work ASP & Co. does is different because the relationship to place is different. Ashton doesn't arrive with a shot list. He arrives with time — and stays long enough to see what a place looks like before it's performing for a camera. The waterfront at first light. The Southside block that has outlasted three waves of development. The light off Tampa Bay at the end of a day that had nowhere to be.

Every portfolio, every love letter, is a visual argument: that coastal destinations with cultural depth deserve to be seen whole — not just their surfaces, but their histories, their contradictions, their people, their particular quality of light.

The stories are the spotlight. The love letters are the record. Ashton is simply the one who keeps showing up.

ASP & Co. works on retainer with experiential businesses, tourism boards, and cultural institutions. If the work resonates — if you recognize your place in what we document — we'd like to hear from you.
